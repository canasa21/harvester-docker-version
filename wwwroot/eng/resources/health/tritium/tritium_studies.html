Tritium studies

Tritium is an isotope of hydrogen produced naturally from the interaction of cosmic rays. It is also a by-product of nuclear power generation, including in Canada's fleet of CANDU (Canada Deuterium Uranium) reactors. This has allowed Canada to develop considerable expertise in the research and management of tritium.

Tritium Studies Project

The Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC) has studied tritium extensively. In January 2007, the Commission directed CNSC staff to initiate research studies on tritium releases in Canada, and to study and evaluate the practices of Canadian tritium processing facilities with respect to international standards. In response, the CNSC led several research initiatives as part of the Tritium Studies Project, which has helped shape Canadian regulatory oversight of tritium processing and tritium releases.

The following studies and documents resulted from the Tritium Studies Project:

  • Tritium Studies Project: Health Effects of Tritium
  • Standards and Guidelines for Tritium in Drinking Water
  • Investigation of the Environmental Fate of Tritium in the Atmosphere
  • Tritium Releases and Dose Consequences in Canada in 2006
  • Evaluation of Facilities Handling Tritium
  • Tritium Activity in Garden Produce from Pembroke in 2007 and Dose to the Public
  • Health Effects, Dosimetry and Radiological Protection of Tritium
  • Tritium Studies Project Synthesis Report
    • Implementation of Recommendations from the Tritium Studies Synthesis Report
  • Environmental Fate of Tritium in Soil and Vegetation
